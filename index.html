project goal
What charge profile minimizes degradation without sacrificing fast-charge speed?
A lithium-ion cell is charged using a 3-step current profile: C1 → C2 → C3, where each C-rate controls how aggressively current is applied during that step. Higher C-rates charge faster but accelerate two distinct forms of battery degradation — capacity fade (loss of lithium inventory, LLI) and impedance rise (SEI layer growth).
This study used Bayesian optimization to intelligently search the protocol space — running 150 simulated charge cycles to find the combinations of C1, C2, and C3 that best balance all three competing objectives simultaneously. The result is a Pareto front: the set of protocols where no single objective can be improved without sacrificing another.
↑ Maximize Q30 (charge speed) ↓ Minimize LLI (capacity fade) ↓ Minimize SEI (impedance rise)

key findings
01
C2=0 rest step is the single strongest predictor of high-throughput performance
Every one of the top 20 trials by Q30 uses C2=0. Protocols with a rest step average Q30=0.064 Ah vs 0.046 Ah for C2>0 — a 39% mean advantage. BoTorch converged on this structure quickly and exploited it exclusively from trial ~30 onward. The rest step is doing real electrochemical work: it allows reversible lithium to strip from the anode surface, partially resetting the anode's degradation state before the C3 pulse.
Strongest structural finding
02
LLI and SEI are anti-correlated (r = −0.36), not co-moving — a genuine theoretical contribution
High-LLI protocols tend to have lower SEI (e.g., trial 5: LLI=0.018 mol, SEI=57.6 nm). High-SEI protocols tend to have lower LLI (e.g., trial 8: SEI=66.2 nm, LLI=0.007 mol). This means minimizing one without tracking the other would yield misleading results. The 3-objective formulation was not redundant — it exposed a real physical tradeoff between two distinct degradation mechanisms driven by different submodels. This anti-correlation is an independent result worth reporting.
Revises prior assumption of co-movement
03
Back-loading works because the rest step depletes the anode, not because high-SoC is inherently safe
The pattern C1=low→C2=0→C3=high is not "load max current at high SoC." It is: charge moderately, rest (allowing lithium stripping to reduce surface Li concentration and partially reverse plating from C1), then deliver a strong final pulse to a partially reset anode. In NMC-Gr the graphite anode is most vulnerable during staging transitions (LiC₁₂→LiC₆) around 50–70% SoC — a rest step before C3 can shift the effective anode SoC downward, reducing the driving force for further plating during C3. This mechanism depends on the reversible plating submodel and is specific to graphite-anode chemistries with distinct staging. It would not generalize to irreversible plating models or to LFP.
Chemistry- and model-specific — not a general principle
04
The most actionable new discovery: 50% LLI reduction at only 6% throughput cost
Trial 134 (1.83C→0C→2.23C): Q30=0.0846 Ah (−5.9% vs baseline), LLI=0.0067 mol (−52%), SEI=64.5 nm (+11%). Trial 2 (2C→0C→2C): Q30=0.0833 Ah (−7.3%), LLI=0.0051 mol (−64%), SEI=64.8 nm. These protocols sacrifice modest throughput and accept modestly higher SEI growth, but cut capacity-fade-related degradation in half. If cycle life rather than charge speed is the design objective, this region of the Pareto front is the real discovery — not the high-throughput end near the baseline.
Most actionable new finding
05
The baseline (3C→0C→3C) is genuinely non-dominated — no trial strictly beats it on all three objectives
Zero trials simultaneously achieve Q30 ≥ 0.0899 Ah, LLI ≤ 0.01406 mol, and SEI ≤ 58.22 nm. The best multi-objective competitor is trial 146 (2.89C→0C→3C): Q30=0.0915 Ah (+1.8%), but LLI=0.0151 mol (+7.4%) and SEI=59.2 nm (+1.7%). The optimizer found the same qualitative protocol, slightly retuned, with only marginal and mixed objective improvements. The practical conclusion holds: added complexity was not worth the return at the high-throughput end of the Pareto front.
Confirms practical conclusion
06
The C1=3C regime is voltage-cutoff saturated, not degradation-limited
All 25+ trials with C1=3C and C2>0 converge to exactly Q30=0.0417 Ah regardless of C2 or C3. C1=3C immediately drives the cell to its upper voltage cutoff, the step terminates early, and subsequent steps contribute little additional charge. This is a model artifact that wastes optimization budget. The only escape is C2=0 — skipping a non-zero second step entirely — which is how the baseline (trial 0) achieves Q30=0.090 Ah. BoTorch correctly learned to avoid C1=3C+C2>0 after the early Sobol phase.
